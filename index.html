PhD Research: Adaptive Authentication and Residual Risk

My PhD research investigates how residual risks arise in adaptive authentication systems and how these risks can be identified, quantified, and mitigated. Adaptive authentication systems select authentication methods based on contextual factors such as location, device, network security, time, user role, and asset sensitivity. However, even after an authentication method is selected, some residual risks may remain.

My work explores how authentication and authorization can work together to reduce residual risk while maintaining useful access. The research combines contextual modelling, fuzzy requirements, threat modelling, SMT/Z3-based reasoning, and machine learning-based risk assessment.

Research Questions

  • How can residual risks be identified and quantified in adaptive authentication systems?
  • How can authentication and authorization work together to mitigate residual risks?
  • How do contextual factors and authentication attacks contribute to residual risk?
  • How can machine learning support adaptive authentication and residual risk assessment?

Current Model

I am developing a residual risk model that links adaptive authentication decisions with dynamic authorization. The model uses contextual factors, authentication method properties, asset sensitivity, attack likelihood, and permission levels to compute residual risk and select suitable access configurations.
